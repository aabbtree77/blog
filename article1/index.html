Compilers

  • Ark (2015): A static non-GC language written with Go and LLVM: FFI to C, OpenGL demo.

  • mini-js (2017): “Minimal self-hosted JavaScript compiler in 1k lines of code.”

  • Monkey (2018): A minimal Js-like language implemented with a recursive descent parser, in Go. Lots of following!

  • Mugo (2021): A subset of Go compiled to the x86-64 assembly, in Go.

Impressive Software Projects

  • Ubuntu 22.04 desktop OS. Starts out in 2004, matures around 2020: NVIDIA drivers out of the box, GNOME 40 and Wayland, dxvk (1.10.3) pushes the PC games to another level.

    Minor weaknesses: deb vs snap, Flutter in 24.04, remote desktop connections, the games which rely on ms-store and xbox win libs (Forza Horizon…), TomTom, Unreal, CAD, extremely fragile “sudo apt upgrade”, buggy fresh releases.

    Have a dual HD/SSD drive setup where most of your data will reside on a slow HD drive and the system on a faster SSD, with an Ubuntu USB stick nearby to reinstall the whole thing.

  • Chromium/Chrome, Viber, Blender run natively on Ubuntu.

  • Spintires run via wine.

  • app.diagrams.net (draw.io) to make diagrams and presentations.

  • golibp2p. See my IoT project for some uses and links.

  • F#. The best static GC language that nobody uses. Other solid efforts by M$: TypeScript, VS Code.

  • Nim: The best static non-GC language that nobody uses.

  • Python, conda, matplotlib, pytorch.

  • TypeScript and React for the web and desktop GUIs. Still lacking a decent metaframework unless one is into Next.js.

  • AnKi 3D engine. More than a million lines of C++ and Vulkan compile cleanly on Ubuntu.

  • Chess software: Fruit, lichess.org, lc0, Stockfish, TCEC, CounterGo, Arena Chess GUI which is written with the GTK2, in Free Pascal (Lazarus)!

Puzzles

  • The box method and its “inverse” discovered in “Al-Jabr” by Al-Khwarizmi, the 9th century.

  • Dimensional analysis. What is the temperature at the center of the Sun, given the star’s mass, radius, the gravitational constant, and assuming it is a ball of hydrogen? The answer is 20 million kelvins. See B.N. Ivanov. Laws of Physics (1986, in Russian), page 286. This is also roughly the temperature of the entire universe around 100 - 1000 seconds after the Big Bang, when there are no atoms and stars yet.
